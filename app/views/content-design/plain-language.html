Content design {% markdown %} # {{ title }} Additional guidance for content designers to understand how to meet the [DfE Plain Language standard](https://standards.education.gov.uk/standard/plain-language) ## Plain English and plain language [Plain English](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/content-design/writing-for-gov-uk#plain-english) is a set of principles used to write clearly and accurately, such as using short sentences. Plain language modifies those techniques to suit the needs of the user and adapts to findings from user research. ## Use the language your users use Sometimes, as a government department, we need to use jargon. If there is a proven user or business need to use jargon, then it is right to use it. For example, when writing for an audience with specialist knowledge. However, when we do use technical terminology or jargon, we must also provide a plain language description of what it means when we first use it. For example: > ‘You’ll receive training based on the early career framework (ECF) as part of your induction period. This is called ECF-based training. > > ECF-based training provides a bridge between your initial teacher training (ITT) and your teaching career. Your ITT helps you qualify as a teacher, and the ECF-based training helps you further develop your teaching practice and working habits.’ Use the following tools to help you meet the standard: - depending on your audience, you may want to check the reading age grade, or score. You can do this by using a readability tool, such as Hemingway, or the Flesch-Kincaid reading grade and reading ease (in Word) - use the '[how many people](https://accessibility.education.gov.uk/app/how-many-people)' tool to understand how many users may have accessibility needs that use your service - review Home Office guidance for [designing for people with limited English](https://design.homeoffice.gov.uk/content-style-guide/designing-for-limited-english#use-clear-language) {% endmarkdown %}
